Choking

Choking occurs when an object, usually food or a small item, blocks the airway, preventing normal breathing. If not treated immediately, choking can lead to suffocation.

Recognizing Choking

  • Be unable to speak, cough, or breathe
  • Clutch their throat (universal choking sign)
  • Have blue-tinted lips or face (due to lack of oxygen)
  • Make high-pitched wheezing sounds or be completely silent

If the person is coughing forcefully, encourage them to keep coughing. If they cannot cough, speak, or breathe, act immediately using the Heimlich maneuver.

The Heimlich Maneuver

The Heimlich maneuver is a lifesaving technique used to dislodge an obstruction from the airway. It varies based on the person's age.

Watch: Heimlich Maneuver

For Adults

  • Stand behind the person and wrap your arms around their waist.
  • Make a fist with one hand and place it just above their belly button.
  • Grab your fist with your other hand and perform quick, inward and upward thrusts.
  • Repeat until the object is expelled or the person becomes unresponsive.

For Children (Ages 1-8)

  • Same technique as for adults but with reduced force.
  • Kneel behind the child if necessary.
  • Perform gentler abdominal thrusts using less force than on an adult.
  • Stop when the object is dislodged or if the child becomes unconscious.

For Infants (Under 1 Year Old)

  • Lay the baby face-down along your forearm, supporting their head and neck.
  • Give 5 firm back blows between the shoulder blades.
  • If the object is not dislodged, turn the infant onto their back.
  • Give 5 chest thrusts using two fingers, pushing down about 1.5 inches.
  • Repeat until the object is expelled or the infant becomes unresponsive.

For Yourself (Self-Heimlich)

  • Make a fist and place it above your belly button.
  • Grab your fist with your other hand and press inward and upward sharply.
  • Lean over a sturdy object (chair, counter, railing) and push your abdomen forcefully against it.
  • Repeat until the object is expelled.

When to Seek Medical Help

  • If the person becomes unconscious.
  • If choking persists despite Heimlich attempts.
  • After successful removal of the object, especially in infants or children, as internal injury may have occurred.
